## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

### Conceptual Understanding
- Understand how maps have been used historically and in contemporary society to represent, construct, and contest space
- Analyze how technology shapes our relationship with human space and place
- Critically evaluate bias and power dynamics in spatial data and mapping practices
- Recognize the role of maps in social justice and advocacy

### Technical Skills
- Use OpenStreetMap (OSM) to contribute to and analyze crowdsourced geographic data
- Apply GIS tools (ArcGIS Online) to spatial analysis and visualization
- Create interactive web-based maps using ESRI Storymaps
- Work with spatial and textual data using R Markdown notebooks
- Assess data quality and identify gaps in geographic coverage

### Research and Analysis
- Conduct geospatial analysis on both historical and contemporary data
- Interpret data within familiar urban and regional contexts
- Develop critical perspectives on the epistemology of maps and mapmaking
- Synthesize multiple sources of geographic and textual information

### Collaboration and Communication
- Work effectively in project-based learning environments
- Communicate spatial analysis findings through multiple media (written, visual, interactive)
- Engage in citizen science and community-based research
- Present findings clearly to diverse audiences

### Data Literacy
- Understand the assumptions embedded in spatial datasets
- Recognize limitations and biases in crowdsourced geographic data
- Ask critical questions about data collection, representation, and interpretation
- Use data to tell stories about places, communities, and human trajectories
